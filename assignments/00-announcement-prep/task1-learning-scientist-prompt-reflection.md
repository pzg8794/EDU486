## AI Image Prompt Used

Create a warm, high-resolution illustration of an adult teacher as a learning scientist for a graduate STEM education course. The person is in a community classroom with a tablet, notebook, small water sample jar, sticky notes, and student-made environmental evidence on the wall. The visual mood is thoughtful, justice-centered, culturally sustaining, and accessible. Show the teacher observing, listening, and documenting learning rather than lecturing. Include subtle visual themes of science, technology, community, and care. No text, no logos, no school names, no children shown, no photorealistic identifiable person.

## Learning Scientist Characteristics

- Observes before interpreting.
- Treats youth questions and community knowledge as evidence.
- Designs with technology, but does not assume technology is always the best answer.
- Documents learning, participation, access, and motivation.
- Revises plans based on what students actually do and say.
- Notices whose knowledge is centered, whose is missing, and who has access to participate.
- Uses science and technology for action, not only for classroom performance.

## Reflection

This image aligns with the role I expect in EDU486 because the syllabus frames teachers as people who study the impact of their choices on student learning, participation, and motivation. The course is not just about using tools in STEM. It asks us to work with Freedom Scholars, learn from community and place, design camp experiences, revise based on youth feedback, and communicate what we learned as learning scientists.

The image also presses against a simpler version of teaching where the teacher is mainly a deliverer of content. In this class, the teacher has to be a listener, designer, observer, facilitator, and critic of technology. That is exciting for me because it connects to my Puzzle Plan and PhD goals around evidence justice, inclusive AI, and equitable learning systems. It also challenges me because a learning scientist cannot hide behind a polished lesson plan. If the tool adds barriers, if the activity does not connect to youth knowledge, or if the data misses what matters, I have to notice that and change the design.

My commitment for this course is to treat technology as a design material, not a default solution. I want to document what students are able to notice, build, explain, question, and revise when the learning environment gives them real agency and access.
