# Invisible Invaders Sand Investigation Guide

**Team:** Piter Garcia and Aastha<br>
**Camp:** Get Real! Science Camp - Invisible Invaders<br>
**Use:** Collect, sieve, density-separate, filter, and examine Charlotte Beach sand with campers<br>
**Current through:** July 22, 2026

> **TECHNICALLY ACCURATE NAME:** We are not filtering sand. We are **sieving the sand, using concentrated saltwater to density-separate material, and filtering the upper saltwater layer** after the heavier sand settles.

> **EVIDENCE LIMIT:** This camp method can reveal and count **suspected fibers, fragments, films, foams, or pellets**. Visual appearance and floating behavior do not chemically confirm that a particle is plastic. Our results are educational screening evidence, not a regulatory measurement or a health test.

## Start Here: The Whole Process

**LABEL -> COLLECT -> SIEVE -> SORT -> ADD BRINE -> MIX -> SETTLE OVERNIGHT -> FILTER THE UPPER LIQUID -> DRY COVERED -> TAPE-LIFT -> MAGNIFY -> RECORD -> CLAIM + LIMIT**

Use this as a three-part investigation rather than rushing an authentic sample:

| Camp moment | What campers do | Approximate time |
| --- | --- | ---: |
| **Field Friday** | Collect, label, map, and seal three consistent sand samples | 10 minutes within the Friday routine |
| **Monday** | Sieve, sort visible material, add prepared brine, mix, and begin settling | 25-35 minutes |
| **Tuesday** | Observe the layers without disturbing them, then filter the upper liquid | 15-25 minutes |
| **Wednesday** | Examine the dried filters, make tape-lift slides, record suspected particles, compare the blank, and revise the model | 30-45 minutes |

If the authentic filters will not be dry in time, show campers a clearly labeled **practice or previously prepared filter** for the microscope routine. Do not substitute it silently for their sample.

## What Campers Are Learning

1. **A sample represents only a small part of a larger place.** A consistent collection method makes comparisons more meaningful.
2. **Sieves separate materials by size.** Large material stays on larger mesh; smaller material passes to finer mesh.
3. **Salt changes the density of water.** Concentrated saltwater can support some lower-density materials while denser sand settles.
4. **Density separation is incomplete.** Some plastics are denser than saltwater, and weathering or material attached to a particle can change whether it floats.
5. **A filter captures suspended solids; it does not identify them.** Natural material, airborne fibers, and suspected plastics may all remain on the paper.
6. **Scientific claims must match the evidence.** We can report what this sample and method showed while naming what remains uncertain.

## Connection To Invisible Invaders

- 🟩 **EARTH SYSTEMS:** Sand, air, water, living things, weather, and human activity are connected.
- 🟧 **WATER:** Water can carry material, and the saltwater model lets us investigate movement and separation.
- 🟦 **LIVING ORGANISMS:** Finding a suspected particle in an environmental sample raises questions about possible encounter pathways; it does not prove exposure or harm.
- 🟪 **HUMAN INFRASTRUCTURE:** Products, roads, drains, wastewater, waste systems, monitoring, and policy influence where particles come from and where they move.

## Adult Preparation Before Camp

### 1. Pilot the complete method

Run one practice sample through every step before guiding campers. Confirm:

- the chosen sand amount and brine volume leave at least one-third of the jar empty for mixing;
- lids do not leak;
- the sieve stack, funnels, and filter paper fit;
- filtering does not take longer than the available block;
- filters dry under a loose foil cover;
- clear tape adheres to a dry filter and remains readable under the available microscope; and
- the camera, software, and paper backup all work.

### 2. Prepare concentrated saltwater

The transcript describes adding salt to water and mixing before adding sand. For a repeatable, no-heat camp preparation:

1. Measure **300 g sodium chloride for each 1.0 L of distilled or deionized water**. A small batch uses **30 g salt per 100 mL water**.
2. Add the salt gradually to water in a clean glass container.
3. Stir or cap and shake until the solution is clear. If a small amount will not dissolve, let it settle and decant only the clear brine.
4. Label the container **LAB SALTWATER - NOT FOR DRINKING**, with the salt mass, water volume, preparer's initials, and date.
5. Keep it covered until use.

Do not call the solution exactly 5 M unless it was prepared to a measured final solution volume. NOAA's full laboratory method uses approximately 5 M sodium chloride with controlled laboratory heating; camp uses a simpler room-temperature concentrated brine and records how it was made.

### 3. Optional density check

If the balance and graduated cylinder are ready, campers can help verify the brine:

1. Tare a clean container.
2. Measure exactly 50.0 mL of brine.
3. Weigh it in grams.
4. Calculate **density = mass / 50.0 mL**.
5. Record the result instead of assuming every batch is identical.

The teaching point is comparative: concentrated saltwater is denser than fresh water. The class does not need to force the result to a particular number.

### 4. Establish one camp processing standard

Pilot this starting standard and then use the same values for SAMPLE 1-3:

- **100 mL of the combined bottom two sieve fractions** per processing jar
- **200 mL prepared brine** per jar
- at least **one-third empty headspace**
- **20 slow inversions followed by 30 seconds of controlled shaking**
- **overnight settling**, with start and stop times recorded

If the jar size or available sample requires a different ratio, Piter, Aastha, and the instructors must choose the replacement during the pilot and write it on every data sheet. Do not change the ratio for only one sample.

## Safety And Contamination Rules

1. **Nothing is tasted.** Lab salt, saltwater, sand, filters, and particles are not food.
2. **Wear goggles during mixing and pouring.** Use well-fitting gloves or a clean-tool, touch-free route.
3. **Adults check every lid before a camper mixes a jar.** Mix over a tray with two hands.
4. **Adults control broken glass, spills, and disposal.** Campers step back and tell an adult.
5. **Do not heat saltwater with campers.** Do not add hydrogen peroxide, iron catalyst, KOH, bleach, dyes, solvents, or other digestion chemicals to this camp procedure.
6. **Do not use a flame or hot-needle test.** It creates safety, air-quality, and identification problems.
7. **Cover samples whenever the procedure does not require them to be open.** Airborne fibers are evidence contamination, not a camper mistake.
8. **Use one clean tool per sample or clean tools completely between samples.** Never move a spoon directly from SAMPLE 1 to SAMPLE 2.
9. **Record clothing and glove colors.** Similar fibers on the filter and clothing are a contamination question to document.
10. **Contain every suspected particle.** Do not wash captured material back into the environment or an unapproved drain.

## Complete Procedure And Teaching Script

### Phase 1 - Label Before Opening Anything

**Facilitator does**

1. Put each sample on its own tray.
2. Label the processing jar, receiving jar, filter-paper envelope or foil cover, slides, and data sheet with the same ID.
3. Prepare **BLANK** exactly like a sample but without sand.
4. Record date, team, sample site, collector, processing people, and clothing/glove colors.

**Say:** "A label protects the story of the sample. If we lose the label, we lose where the evidence came from."

**Ask:** "What information would another scientist need in order to repeat what we did?"

### Phase 2 - Collect Consistently At The Beach

This phase is already embedded in the [Friday Anchoring Phenomenon Routine](friday-anchoring-phenomenon-routine.md).

1. Match SAMPLE 1-3 to the map before collecting.
2. Place the sampling square at the instructor-approved site.
3. Use a clean metal scoop to collect the upper approximately **1 cm** in a systematic pattern across the square. Use the same row pattern, depth, and scoop amount at each site.
4. Record site, time, weather, surface condition, nearby activity, tool, and anything that might contaminate the sample.
5. Seal the jar before moving to the next site.
6. Keep SAMPLE 1-3 separate from the prepared MODEL A-D jars.

**Say:** "We are collecting one small part of the beach. Our sample can answer a question about this jar under this method; it cannot speak for every grain of sand on the beach."

### Phase 3 - Sieve By Size

1. Check whether the sand can move freely through the sieves. If it is wet and clumped, air-dry it under a loose clean foil cover. Do not change the method for only one sample.
2. Stack the metal sieves from largest mesh to smallest mesh over the collection pan. Read and record each labeled mesh opening before beginning.
3. Add one measured sample. Stop before the top sieve is halfway full.
4. Close the stack. One camper holds it level while another times **30 seconds** of the same controlled motion used in the pilot.
5. Open one sieve at a time over its labeled tray.
6. Use tweezers to move visible material to **natural/uncertain** or **suspected human-made** areas.
7. Record leaves, twigs, shells, stones, and each suspected fiber, fragment, film, foam, or pellet.
8. Dr. April directed us to proceed with the bottom two sieve fractions and treat them the same. For our three-site camp comparison, combine those bottom two fractions from each site in that site's processing jar and write **COMBINED BOTTOM TWO FRACTIONS** on the jar and data sheet. If the instructors provide enough jars and time to keep the fractions separate, apply the same complete protocol to both and record the change before processing any sample.

**Say:** "The sieve tells us about size, not identity. Something small is not automatically plastic, and something natural can be small too."

**Ask:** "What stayed above? What moved below? What did mesh size cause?"

### Phase 4 - Add Brine And Mix

1. Place the pilot-approved amount of sieved sand in its labeled processing jar.
2. Measure the pilot-approved volume of prepared brine and add it to the jar.
3. Leave at least one-third of the jar empty.
4. Seal the jar. An adult checks the lid.
5. Record the sand amount, brine volume, brine batch, and starting time.
6. Over a tray, complete the same mixing routine for every sample: **20 slow inversions, then 30 seconds of controlled shaking**.
7. Process the BLANK beside the samples using the same brine, open-air exposure, tools, timing, funnel, and filter steps, but no sand.

**Say:** "Salt makes the water denser. Some lower-density material may remain higher while much of the denser sand settles. This is a separation test, not a plastic detector."

**Ask before mixing:** "Where do you predict the sand and different particles will be after the jar rests? Why?"

### Phase 5 - Settle Undisturbed

1. Put each sealed jar upright in its labeled tray on a stable shelf away from traffic and direct heat.
2. Record the settling start time.
3. Do not move, tap, open, or compare jars by picking them up.
4. Let the jars settle overnight.
5. The next day, observe before touching. Photograph or draw the surface, water column, and bottom layer.
6. Record the end time and total settling time.

**Say:** "We are giving gravity time to separate material. A particle that sinks is not proven natural, and a particle that floats is not proven plastic."

**Ask:** "What changed while nobody touched the jar? What evidence supports your explanation?"

### Phase 6 - Filter The Upper Saltwater Layer

Process BLANK first, then SAMPLE 1-3.

1. Set a clean porcelain or glass funnel over the matching glass receiving jar.
2. Use clean tweezers to place the matching filter paper into the funnel. Avoid touching the filter surface.
3. Without shaking or remixing, bring the processing jar to the tray.
4. Carefully pour the **upper saltwater layer** through the filter. Stop before settled sand enters the funnel.
5. If floating material sticks to the upper jar wall, use a small, recorded rinse of distilled water to move it onto the same filter.
6. Let all liquid pass through. Do not press, scrape, or rub the filter.
7. Use tweezers to transfer the filter to its labeled clean surface.
8. Cover loosely with clean aluminum foil and air-dry for 12-24 hours.
9. Keep the filtrate and settled sand contained until an instructor approves disposal.

**Say:** "The filter catches suspended material. It can catch natural material, airborne fibers, and suspected plastics. The filter does not tell us which is which."

**Ask:** "What passed through? What stayed on the paper? What can the filter answer, and what can it not answer?"

### Phase 7 - Make A Tape-Lift Slide

Only use a completely dry filter.

1. Label the slide with sample ID, date, filter fraction, and team.
2. Divide the observation plan into four filter areas: top-left, top-right, bottom-left, and bottom-right. Do not write across the evidence surface.
3. Press one clean piece of crystal-clear tape lightly onto the first area; do not rub.
4. Lift once and place the tape flat on the labeled slide.
5. Repeat with fresh tape or a new slide for each planned area.
6. Record which filter area each tape lift represents.

**Say:** "A systematic path helps us look beyond the first interesting thing we notice."

### Phase 8 - Magnify, Classify, And Count

1. Begin with the BLANK slide, then examine SAMPLE 1-3.
2. Use approximately 40X magnification when available, or record the actual magnification used.
3. Scan in a consistent row-by-row path rather than jumping around the slide.
4. For every suspected particle, record:
   - form: fiber, fragment, film, foam, or pellet;
   - color;
   - longest visible length in millimeters when measurement is possible;
   - slide area or tape-lift number;
   - confidence: **likely natural**, **uncertain**, or **suspected human-made**; and
   - one reason for the classification.
5. A second camper or facilitator checks at least one slide area. Record disagreements rather than erasing them.
6. Use the microscope camera or shared display so nobody must crowd an eyepiece.

**Say:** "We are describing what the particle looks like. Without chemical analysis, our strongest honest label is suspected plastic."

**Ask:** "What observation supports your classification? What else could this particle be?"

### Phase 9 - Compare The Blank Before Making A Claim

1. Count and describe material found on the BLANK filter.
2. Compare blank colors and forms with SAMPLE 1-3.
3. If a similar fiber appears in the blank and samples, flag it as a possible handling or airborne contaminant.
4. Do not automatically subtract the blank count. Report the blank beside the sample and explain how it affects confidence.

**Say:** "The blank helps us notice what our tools, air, clothing, or handling may have added. It does not accuse anyone; it helps us improve the method."

### Phase 10 - Make A Claim That Fits The Evidence

Use this frame:

> **In SAMPLE ___, processed with ___ mL sand and ___ mL brine after ___ hours of settling, we observed ___ suspected fibers/fragments/films/foams/pellets. The BLANK contained ___. This evidence suggests ___. We cannot yet conclude ___ because ___.**

Acceptable camp claim:

> "Under our camp method, SAMPLE 2's filter contained three suspected blue fibers. The blank contained one similar blue fiber, so some of the evidence may have entered during handling or from the air. Visual observation cannot confirm the material."

Claims the method does **not** support:

- "There are exactly three microplastics at Charlotte Beach."
- "Every floating particle was plastic."
- "No floating particles means there was no plastic."
- "This particle came from a particular person, neighborhood, product, or company."
- "This sample proves that someone was exposed or harmed."

## Camper Roles And Access Routes

No role owns the science. Campers may switch, share, pass, or return.

| Role | Scientific contribution | Access route |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Label protector | Matches every jar, filter, slide, and data sheet | Seated, low-movement |
| Sampler | Uses the consistent field pattern | Movement or partnered |
| Sieve operator | Runs the same timed motion | Standing, seated-table, or partner-assisted |
| Timer | Protects mixing, settling, and filtering time | Visual, audio, or tactile timer |
| Brine measurer | Reads or verifies volume | Large-print scale or partner reading |
| Filter monitor | Watches flow and tells the pourer when to stop | Touch-free observation |
| Evidence photographer | Records layers, labels, and non-identifying evidence | Camera or tablet |
| Microscope navigator | Moves through the slide systematically | Eyepiece, shared screen, or enlarged still image |
| Evidence recorder | Draws, writes, types, dictates, points, or records audio | Multiple communication modes |
| Skeptic/checker | Asks what else a particle could be and checks the blank | Verbal or nonverbal |
| Seated coordinator | Tracks the whole sequence and next step | Low-energy route |

Facilitators show the full visual sequence while giving **one spoken direction at a time**. Offer goggles that fit, several glove sizes, a no-touch route, seated work, quiet observation, a shared microscope display, breaks, home language, partner response, and optional public speaking.

## Troubleshooting

| Problem | Do this | Do not do this |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Wet sand clogs the sieve | Cover and air-dry all comparable samples; record the change | Force only one sample through or add unrecorded water |
| Salt remains at the bottom of the brine container | Let it settle and use the clear upper brine; record the recipe | Pour salt crystals into one sample only |
| Processing jar leaks | Stop, contain the spill, transfer only with adult approval, and flag the sample | Keep shaking or hide the loss |
| Sand begins entering the funnel | Stop pouring; record it; use a new filter if flow is blocked | Scrape or squeeze the filter |
| Filter flows very slowly | Let gravity work; cover the setup; use the same filter type for all samples | Poke holes or press material through |
| Filter is still wet | Keep it covered and wait, or use a clearly labeled practice slide | Apply tape and pretend the poor transfer is sample evidence |
| Fibers appear in the blank | Report them and lower confidence in similar sample fibers | Delete the blank or automatically subtract counts |
| Microscope cannot connect | Use a hand lens, enlarged still image, drawing, and preserved filter | Treat technology failure as learner failure |
| Campers disagree about a particle | Record both classifications and the evidence for each | Let the fastest or loudest answer become fact |

## Cleanup And Disposal

1. Keep every filter, tape lift, and suspected particle labeled and covered.
2. Place used filters and tape in a labeled sealed solid-waste container or archive envelope.
3. Do not pour unfiltered sample liquid, sand, or captured solids down a drain.
4. Follow Dr. April's or the lab's approved disposal route for filtered brine, environmental sand, and glassware wash water.
5. Wash reusable glass, porcelain, and metal tools with the approved laboratory method; rinse with distilled water; air-dry covered.
6. Clean one sample station at a time to prevent label swaps.
7. Photograph completed data sheets and preserve the original sheets.
8. Wash hands after gloves are removed and before eating.

## Camper Quick Guide

### Day 1: Separate

1. **MATCH** the sample ID on the jar, map, and data sheet.
2. **MEASURE** the same amount of the combined bottom two sieve fractions used for the other samples.
3. **SIEVE** for the same amount of time.
4. **SORT** what you see as natural/uncertain or suspected human-made.
5. **ADD** the same amount of prepared saltwater.
6. **MIX** with the class routine over a tray.
7. **PREDICT** what will rise, stay in the middle, or sink.
8. **SETTLE** overnight without touching the jar.

### Day 2: Filter

1. **NOTICE** the layers before moving the jar.
2. **SET** clean filter paper in the matching funnel.
3. **POUR** only the upper saltwater layer; leave settled sand behind.
4. **COVER** the filter and let it dry.

### Day 3: Examine

1. **LABEL** the slide.
2. **LIFT** evidence from one planned filter area with clear tape.
3. **SCAN** the slide in rows.
4. **DESCRIBE** form, color, size, and confidence.
5. **COMPARE** with the BLANK.
6. **CLAIM** only what the evidence supports.
7. **NAME** what is still unknown.
8. **REVISE** the class model or add a new question.

## Scientific Foundation

- [NOAA: Laboratory Methods for the Analysis of Microplastics in the Marine Environment](https://repository.library.noaa.gov/view/noaa/10296/noaa_10296_DS1.pdf) supports density separation, overnight settling, transfer of floating solids, covered drying, and microscope examination. NOAA's complete analytical method includes heated and reactive laboratory steps that are **not part of this youth camp protocol**.
- [EPA: National Coastal Condition Assessment Field Operations Manual](https://www.epa.gov/system/files/documents/2025-08/ncca_2020_fom_version_1.3_final_20250730.pdf) supports glass jars, metal sampling tools, recording clothing and glove colors, minimizing synthetic-fiber exposure, and collecting an air blank when sediment is exposed.
- [EPA: Handbook for Citizen Science Quality Assurance and Documentation](https://www.epa.gov/sites/default/files/2019-03/documents/508_csqapphandbook_3_5_19_mmedits.pdf) supports consistent written procedures, field and equipment blanks, replicate or split samples, training, and matching the strength of claims to the intended use and quality of the data.
