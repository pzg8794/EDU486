## Proposed Poster Question

How can technology support youth in creating environmental evidence without replacing community knowledge, place-based noticing, or student agency?

## One-Sentence Claim

In Planet Protectors Camp, technology becomes justice-centered when it helps youth notice, organize, question, and communicate evidence, but it must be designed with access, local knowledge, and tool limitations visible from the beginning.

## Poster Sections

### 1. Context and Problem

Planet Protectors Camp asks educators to design STEM learning that is responsive to youth, community, environmental action, and justice-centered pedagogy. A central tension is that technology can deepen participation, but it can also distract from place, add access barriers, or make data seem more authoritative than lived experience.

### 2. Frameworks

- JuST Framework: build community, plan for meaningfulness and justice, elicit student culture, revise with diverse/local expertise, use learning to make the world better
- PICRAT: ask whether students are passive, interactive, or creative and whether technology replaces, amplifies, or transforms learning
- Community cultural wealth and local knowledge: students and communities bring real assets into science learning
- EQUITAS habit from my broader Puzzle Plan: equity, universality, transparency, inclusion, access

### 3. Camp Design Move

Students investigate plastic pollution or water-related environmental questions by moving through a community evidence cycle:

1. Notice a place or problem.
2. Ask a question.
3. Collect or document evidence.
4. Use technology to organize or represent patterns.
5. Critique what the tool shows and misses.
6. Communicate a message for action.

### 4. Evidence To Include

Use only de-identified and appropriate artifacts:

- student question cards or anonymized excerpts
- non-identifying photos of materials, maps, charts, or posters
- facilitator observation notes
- before/after revision examples
- student reflections about what the technology helped them see
- student reflections about what the technology could not show

### 5. Emerging Insight

The strongest technology integration may be the kind students can question. When youth can explain both the affordance and the limit of a tool, they are not just using technology. They are practicing critical STEM literacy.

### 6. Implications

For teaching:

- Begin with belonging and meaningful questions before tools.
- Build in multiple roles and multiple ways to show learning.
- Treat local knowledge as evidence.
- Make tool limits explicit.
- Use technology for audience, action, and revision, not just presentation polish.

For my PhD/Puzzle Plan:

- This course gives me a community-based version of a larger research question about equitable evidence systems.
- The same questions that matter in AI and diagnostics also matter in classrooms: Whose data counts? Who is missed? Who gets to interpret the pattern? Who benefits from the decision?

## Draft Abstract

This poster examines how technology can support justice-centered STEM learning in a community-based camp setting. Drawing on the JuST Framework, PICRAT, community cultural wealth, and my broader Puzzle Plan work on equitable evidence systems, I study how youth can use accessible tools to notice, organize, question, and communicate environmental evidence. The poster focuses on a Planet Protectors camp design in which students investigate plastic pollution or water-related environmental questions through observation, mapping, data organization, and public communication. Rather than treating technology as a neutral add-on, I ask what the tools make visible, what they hide, and how design choices affect student agency and access. The key insight is that technology becomes more justice-centered when youth can use it creatively and critically: not only to produce a polished product, but to make community knowledge harder to ignore.
