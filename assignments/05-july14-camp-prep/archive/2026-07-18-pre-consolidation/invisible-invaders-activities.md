# Invisible Invaders: Human-Body Phenomenon Lesson Activities

**Team:** Piter Garcia and Aastha

**Current anchoring phenomenon:** **Humans aren’t trash cans, but we have plastics in our bodies.**

**Driving question:** How can plastics from everyday materials become small enough to move through connected systems and reach human bodies, and what changes could reduce those pathways fairly?

This plan replaces the earlier tire-based and parking-lot/classroom lesson focus. Environmental samples may still provide supporting evidence about where particles can be found, but they are not the anchoring phenomenon.

## Design Commitments

- Begin with the human-sample evidence and its limits, not with a predetermined source.
- Let campers model several possible pathways through air, food, water, buildings, and human infrastructure.
- Use the four selected checklist ideas throughout the lesson.
- Treat detection, possible exposure route, prevalence, and health effect as different claims.
- Give youth several advocacy directions and real decision-makers rather than steering everyone to one solution.
- Build bodymind access, sensory safety, language access, energy flexibility, privacy, and multiple identities into every activity.

## Opening Moment: Evidence Pathway Mystery

### Before

- Post the visual agenda: **Notice -> Connect -> Model -> Question -> Choose**.
- Prepare a human silhouette and two short evidence cards: plastic detected in a small blood study and plastic detected in a small lung-tissue study.
- Prepare the evidence-limit card: **Detected in these samples does not mean universal, identify every route, or prove a health effect.**
- Prepare source/pathway cards for clothing, packaging, paint, food and drink containers, classroom materials, indoor dust, air, food, water, buildings, waste systems, inhalation, ingestion, research, and policy.
- Offer image-first, text-first, paper, magnetic, digital, spoken, audio, partner-supported, home-language, seated, low-energy, and environment-only routes.

### During

1. Campers privately or publicly record what the evidence shows, does not show, and makes them wonder.
2. Campers select an everyday plastic-containing material and build a first **before -> during -> after** pathway toward possible human exposure.
3. Groups compare routes and mark each connection as **evidence**, **possible**, or **unknown**.
4. Questions are organized under **sources, becoming small, movement, living bodies, evidence, systems, and action**.

### After

Campers view the five advocacy pathways without selecting a required answer: products and purchasing, indoor air and school materials, food and water systems, community research and monitoring, and policy/environmental justice. They identify which part of the system they might want to investigate or change and what evidence they would need.

## Day 1: Encounter, Model, And Ask

**Question:** What did researchers detect in human samples, what does that evidence mean, and how might plastic reach a body?

**Core work:** Complete the opening moment, build an initial model, begin the question board, and introduce the four-item gotta-have checklist.

**Evidence produced:** evidence-limit sort, initial pathway model, questions, and first checklist connections.

**Access:** Body-content preview; equivalent environment-only route; drawing, text, cards, speech, audio, home language, private response, partner support, seated and low-energy roles.

## Day 2: Investigate Release, Movement, And Scale

**Question:** How can larger plastic-containing materials release particles, and how can connected systems move them?

**Core work:** Use a size ladder; compare several material and pathway cards; examine safe environmental samples, prepared images, or direct-source evidence; trace routes through air, food, water, buildings, and waste systems.

**Evidence produced:** source-release-pathway notes, scale explanation, environmental observations, and revisions connected to checklist ideas 1 and 2.

**Access:** Shared magnified images, printed cards, large diagrams, paper/digital records, clean covered samples, quiet station, movement role, remote-viewing route, and no forced touching or smelling.

## Day 3: Explain Detection, Uncertainty, And Power

**Question:** What can our evidence explain about plastics in human bodies, what remains uncertain, and who controls the systems involved?

**Core work:** Compare the blood and lung studies with FDA and WHO evidence-limit cards; revise models; complete a justice-and-power map; distinguish possible routes from proven routes and detection from health outcomes.

**Evidence produced:** revised causal model, claim/evidence/limit statement, power map, and two possible system points for action.

**Access:** Short direct-source cards, captions, audio, text, image summaries, partner reading, home-language discussion, quiet research, and a non-body-focused evidence route.

## Day 4: Choose, Advocate, And Showcase

**Question:** Which fair change fits our evidence, who can make it, and how will we communicate the request?

**Core work:** Check the final model for all four gotta-have ideas; choose a system point and decision-maker; complete the evidence-to-action planner; create advocacy art, an image-led poster, audio message, captioned video, model, letter, or partner-supported presentation.

**Evidence produced:** final model, gapless explanation, uncertainty statement, justice check, specific request, and communication product.

**Access:** Choice of medium, language, audience visibility, live or prerecorded contribution, individual or shared authorship, private contribution, and presentation role. Scientific reasoning is evaluated separately from polish or public performance.

## Five Advocacy Pathways

| Pathway | Youth question | Possible audience | Feasible product |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Products and purchasing** | Which product or purchasing decisions could reduce unnecessary plastic release? | School purchasing office, camp director, supplier | Comparison, recommendation, illustrated request |
| **Indoor air and materials** | Which school materials or practices deserve investigation or change? | Principal, facilities, custodial team | Material audit, monitoring question, facilities request |
| **Food and water systems** | Where could plastic enter food or water pathways, and who can prevent or monitor it? | Cafeteria, water authority, public-health office | Evidence request, container comparison, prevention proposal |
| **Research and monitoring** | Whose environments and bodies are studied, and whose questions guide research? | University lab, community organization, health department | Youth research question, interview, monitoring request |
| **Policy and environmental justice** | Who receives protection, who bears burdens, and what collective change would be fair? | School board, agency, elected official, public audience | Public comment, audio message, exhibit, advocacy art |

## Shared Scientific Criteria

- The model includes a plastic-containing material, a release mechanism, movement through connected systems, a possible exposure route, human-sample evidence, and an evidence limit.
- The explanation uses all four selected checklist ideas.
- Possible pathways are not presented as proven routes for every detected particle.
- Detection is not presented as proof of a health effect.
- The action reaches someone with power and does not blame youth or families for systems they do not control.
- Different participation routes are judged by the same evidence and reasoning goals.

## Safety And Bodymind Access

- Do not burn plastic, create dust, use unknown powders, or require campers to touch, smell, or taste investigation materials.
- Use sealed or image-based evidence where direct contact is unnecessary.
- Preview human-body content and provide an equivalent environment-only route.
- Build in quiet space, movement breaks, water, seating, one-step directions, predictable transitions, and extra processing time.
- Do not require disclosure of disability, illness, culture, language, poverty, trauma, or family practices.
- Make every digital resource available through a printed or offline route.
