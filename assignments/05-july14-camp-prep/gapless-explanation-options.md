These are complete working causal chains for the three camp themes. They distinguish established mechanisms from uncertainty and connect the science to unequal exposure, decision-making power, youth agency, and accessible evidence. Put only the correct team's version into its shared Google Doc.

## Plankton To People

**Phenomenon:** Microplastics are found in lake and shoreline samples and can move through aquatic food webs, creating possible routes of human exposure.

Plastic objects and synthetic materials enter the environment through litter, stormwater, wastewater, industrial loss, tire wear, clothing fibers, and breakdown of larger waste. Sunlight weakens some plastics, while waves, sand, weather, and repeated abrasion fragment them. Fragmentation makes pieces smaller but does not make the plastic disappear.

Small particles can be carried by wind, runoff, streams, rivers, and currents. The connected Great Lakes and the Genesee River create possible transport routes toward Lake Ontario and Charlotte Beach, while local litter and drainage add nearby sources. Particle size, density, shape, weather, and water movement affect whether particles float, sink, wash ashore, or remain suspended.

Some microplastics overlap in size with food eaten by plankton and other small organisms. Organisms may encounter or ingest particles while feeding. Some particles are expelled; others may remain long enough to affect feeding or move when a predator eats that organism. Repeated predator-prey interactions can transfer particles through a food web. This is not proof that every particle travels in a simple line from plankton to a person, and the degree of accumulation varies by particle, species, and environment.

People can encounter microplastics through food, water, and air. The presence of a particle does not by itself establish a specific health effect; particle chemistry, size, dose, additives, and exposure duration matter, and important health questions remain under study. A scientifically honest model should show both the supported pathway and the uncertainty.

This becomes a justice issue because communities do not have equal control over plastic production, waste infrastructure, water monitoring, cleanup resources, or exposure. Youth and community observations may identify pollution before institutions respond. Solutions can include source reduction, better product and stormwater policy, improved wastewater controls, producer responsibility, monitoring chosen with communities, and public communication, not only asking individuals to pick up litter.

**Evidence campers can use:** watershed maps; shoreline and water samples; particle sorting and microscopy; size, shape, color, and location counts; food-web models; uncertainty labels; community observations; and before/after model revisions.

## Invisible Invaders

**Phenomenon:** Microplastics can be found outdoors and indoors even when people cannot see them with unaided eyes.

Plastics become microscopic through several routes. Tires and road markings abrade during traffic. Synthetic textiles shed fibers during wearing, washing, and drying. Packaging, paint, flooring, furniture, toys, and other products weather or rub against surfaces. Some materials are manufactured at microscopic sizes. These sources continually release particles into air, dust, soil, and water.

Small size makes the particles difficult to notice, not absent. Air movement can suspend and transport light fibers and fragments. Gravity and contact deposit particles in dust and on surfaces. Outdoors, rain and runoff move road and surface particles into soil, drains, streams, and lakes. Indoors, ventilation, cleaning practices, room use, materials, and movement affect where particles collect.

People and other organisms can encounter particles by breathing air, swallowing dust, drinking water, eating food, or touching contaminated surfaces. Detection does not by itself prove harm. Risk depends on particle size, chemistry, concentration, route, duration, and individual context; research is still resolving many health effects. Microscopes, filters, spectroscopy, and careful sampling make different parts of the phenomenon visible, but each method misses some particles and can introduce contamination.

Exposure and protection are unequal. Housing location, proximity to traffic or industry, workplace conditions, building maintenance, ventilation, waste systems, and access to reliable information shape what people encounter and what choices are realistically available. Blaming families for products and environments they do not control hides these systems.

Solutions can act at several levels: reduce unnecessary plastic and shedding at the source; redesign products and tires; strengthen workplace, building, stormwater, and producer rules; improve measurement; and let communities help choose where monitoring occurs and how findings are communicated.

**Evidence campers can use:** paired indoor/outdoor dust or deposition samples; controlled blank samples; microscope images; source-shape comparisons; room or neighborhood maps; counts with uncertainty; and models showing source, transport, deposition, exposure, and intervention.

## Fiber Fighters

**Phenomenon:** Fibers shed from clothing and textiles can move through air, laundry, wastewater, land, and waterways and become part of ecological and human exposure.

Many textiles contain polyester, nylon, acrylic, or other plastics. Friction during manufacturing, wearing, washing, and drying releases tiny fibers. Fabric construction, age, wash conditions, and handling affect shedding. Natural fibers also shed, but synthetic microfibers persist as plastic and may carry dyes or additives.

Laundry water carries fibers into wastewater systems. Treatment plants capture many particles, but not all. Some leave in treated effluent; captured fibers can remain in sludge, and land application can move them into soil and runoff. Dryers, clothing, and household dust also create airborne routes. Rivers and currents transport fibers, while density and attachment to other material influence floating, sinking, or accumulation.

Aquatic and land organisms may ingest or contact fibers. Predators can then encounter particles through prey. People may encounter microfibers through air, dust, water, and food. Presence is not the same as a proven health outcome; size, material, dose, additives, and exposure route matter, and uncertainty should remain visible in the model.

Fast-fashion systems increase production, short use cycles, washing, and textile waste. Environmental burdens and unsafe labor conditions are often displaced onto workers and communities with less decision-making power. Clothing is also identity, culture, protection, and joy, so a justice-centered response cannot shame young people or families for what they can afford or choose to wear.

Actions can include durable design, repair and visible mending, reuse, lower-shedding materials, effective filters, improved wastewater systems, producer responsibility, labor protections, and policy that reduces overproduction. Youth can combine textile science, identity, art, and advocacy to ask manufacturers and institutions for changes.

**Evidence campers can use:** controlled fabric-wash comparisons; filters and microscope images; fiber counts by fabric or condition; flow models from closet to air/wastewater/soil/water; clothing-life-cycle maps; repair or redesign prototypes; and advocacy art with a defined audience.

## Accessibility And Evidence Check

For any theme, scientific rigor does not require one participation mode. Campers can contribute through sample handling, contamination control, photography, drawing, counting, mapping, oral explanation, home-language discussion, data entry, model building, question tracking, art, or audience communication. Provide seated and low-energy roles, non-screen routes, quiet work options, visible steps, and enough time to revise. Assess the causal reasoning and use of evidence, not speed, handwriting, sustained standing, or comfort speaking to a whole group.
