# Invisible Invaders Activity Plan

**Team:** Piter Garcia and Aastha

**Shared planning document:** [Invisible Invaders' Gapless Explanation](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1IFu_y_sYbNeNs1K4J_lD6KS4I30f8wxggX4SS38acRI/edit)

> **Investigation goal:** Campers will use paired parking-lot, classroom, and blank samples to build and revise an evidence-based explanation for how suspected microplastics can reach both places.

This plan preserves Aastha's original decomposition, scale, sampling, air-movement, tire-wear, and poster ideas. It strengthens the causal sequence, aligns the work with the camp sampling protocol, and makes access and safety part of the scientific design.

The route can change without lowering the evidence goal. Campers may observe directly or through a camera, record with paper or technology, communicate through speech or another medium, and take seated or low-energy roles.

## What We Are Keeping From Aastha

- **Banana and plastic comparison:** Use a multi-day observation to ask why biological material and plastic change differently under the same camp conditions. The activity shows different visible rates; it does not prove that plastic never changes.
- **Scale ladder:** Begin with the 5 millimeter microplastic boundary, then move to micrometers and nanometers with a printed scale or zoom sequence. Do not imply that thread width equals one nanometer.
- **Indoor and outdoor samples:** Compare the parking lot and classroom while using a field blank to reveal contamination introduced during collection or processing.
- **Air movement and settling:** Use large, recoverable paper pieces as a model for movement. Make clear that the model represents a mechanism and is not a test of actual microplastic behavior.
- **Tire-wear evidence:** Use photographs, an instructor-prepared sealed sample, or a fully enclosed demonstration instead of having campers make tire dust.
- **Process-of-science poster:** Show the initial model, evidence, blank, revised model, uncertainty, next question, and action rather than only a polished conclusion.

## Non-Negotiable Safety Changes

- Do not have campers sand tires, aerosolize dust, or release loose powder near faces or breathing zones.
- Keep samples covered except during a defined collection or observation window.
- Use clean tools, labels, field blanks, and handwashing to protect both campers and data quality.
- Use the term **suspected microplastics** for visual observations unless polymer testing confirms the material.
- Never ask a camper to trade asthma, pain, fatigue, chemical sensitivity, sensory safety, or mobility needs for participation.

## Four-Day Investigation

### Day 1: Notice, Wonder, And Build The First Model

**Question:** How can something too small to see with our eyes be present in a parking lot and a closed classroom?

**Activities:** Compare the banana and plastic observation jars, use the scale ladder, examine the [USGS microplastics slideshow](https://www.usgs.gov/media/slideshows/microplastics), and build an initial indoor-outdoor model with cards, objects, drawing, speech, text, or home language.

**Evidence produced:** Initial source-and-pathway model, individual notices and wonders, and a class list of claims that need evidence.

**Access:** Preview the agenda; provide image, object, text, and audio entry points; allow quiet observation and nonverbal contribution; define every scale unit beside a visual reference.

### Day 2: Collect Paired Samples And A Blank

**Question:** What evidence would let us compare the parking lot and classroom without confusing environmental particles with contamination from our own process?

**Activities:** Photograph or map possible sources, label three collection surfaces or containers, expose the indoor and outdoor samples for the same planned time, open the field blank only during the collection window, and cover all three together. Follow the team's [sampling protocol](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1HP06TOL2cnycQSgDcRfV57cEUYHuwoHTmVGRABv9tys/edit).

**Evidence produced:** Sample IDs, location and time records, source photographs or maps, field notes, and one contamination-control blank.

**Access:** Offer photographer, mapper, labeler, timer, clean-tools monitor, recorder, and seated coordinator roles. No role requires public speaking, prolonged standing, handwriting, or exclusive control of a device.

### Day 3: Observe, Classify, Compare, And Revise

**Question:** What patterns appear across the indoor, outdoor, and blank samples, and how certain can we be about what we see?

**Activities:** Use a microscope or camera feed to count and describe suspected fibers and fragments by size, color, and shape. Compare all three samples, record contamination concerns, and revise the model to show sources, movement, settling, and uncertainty. Use [EPA's visual-microscopy limits](https://cfpub.epa.gov/si/si_public_record_report.cfm?Lab=CEMM&dirEntryId=361542) and [NOAA laboratory methods](https://marinedebris.noaa.gov/microplastics/laboratory-methods-analysis-microplastics-marine-environment) to distinguish observation from confirmation.

**Evidence produced:** Shared tally, image or drawing record, comparison statement, uncertainty note, and revised model.

**Access:** Pair direct microscope viewing with a camera feed; allow paper tally, typing, dictation, pointing, or partner recording; schedule breaks; keep a clean-air and low-sensory route available.

### Day 4: Explain, Examine Justice, And Advocate

**Question:** What can our evidence support, who is affected by the systems we identified, and what should a real audience understand or change?

**Activities:** Complete the [Invisible Invaders SASSY table](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1yVJ2Ek76Zrgq6mpxN0lT6nqt5FcuZxqEuatsjLsGg8I/edit), compare the first and revised models, map traffic and building factors, and create an image-led poster, short video, audio message, or plastic-art statement. Use [EPA school indoor-air guidance](https://www.epa.gov/iaq-schools/why-indoor-air-quality-important-schools) and the [U.S. school-roadway study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4179205/) without claiming that either source measured our classroom's microplastics.

**Evidence produced:** Gapless explanation, before-and-after model comparison, evidence-limit statement, and public action message.

**Access:** Let campers choose medium, language, audience, visibility, and role. Offer private or pre-recorded contribution, processing time, image-first communication, and shared authorship.

## Shared Data Record

Record the same fields for the parking-lot, classroom, and blank samples:

- sample ID, location, date, start time, end time, and collector roles;
- covered or uncovered status and any event that may have contaminated the sample;
- suspected fiber and fragment counts by color, shape, and approximate size;
- photograph, drawing, or camera record when available;
- what the observation supports, what it complicates, and what it cannot answer.

## Access Routes That Protect Rigor

- **Observe:** direct microscope, camera feed, still image, or partner description.
- **Record:** paper tally, typed form, photograph, drawing, dictation, or sorting cards.
- **Explain:** speech, text, model, annotated image, audio, home language, or co-authored response.
- **Contribute:** mobile field role, seated station role, low-energy role, clean-tools role, or remote observation role.
- **Regulate:** visible agenda, one-step cards, predictable transitions, quiet route, sensory break, and no forced cold calling.

Every route still requires campers to use evidence, compare samples, document uncertainty, and revise an explanation.
