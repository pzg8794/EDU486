## The 60-Second Explanation

1. **MATERIAL AND PERSISTENCE [SCIENCE]:** Most everyday plastics are long chains of molecules designed to resist quick breakdown. Sunlight, oxygen, heat, and repeated friction can weaken an object and split it into smaller pieces, but becoming too small to see is not the same as disappearing. A plastic piece smaller than 5 millimeters is commonly called a microplastic. [NOAA: what microplastics are](https://marinedebris.noaa.gov/what-are-microplastics-0) and [NOAA: how larger plastic fragments](https://response.restoration.noaa.gov/node/1471).

2. **PARKING-LOT SOURCES [SCIENCE]:** Tires grip pavement through friction, and that contact wears material from both the tire and road surface. The resulting tire-road-wear particles can collect on or near pavement, become part of road dust, move in stormwater, or be lifted with airborne dust. Litter, paint, and older plastic objects can add other fragments to the same outdoor sample. [EPA: tire-wear particles and their pathways](https://www.epa.gov/sciencematters/where-rubber-meets-road-epa-researchers-study-environmental-and-health-impacts-tires).

3. **CLASSROOM SOURCES [SCIENCE]:** A classroom creates particles even when its doors are closed: clothing, carpets, curtains, furniture, paint, and plastic supplies experience rubbing and wear. People and objects also carry dust through the room, while doors, windows, and ventilation have connected indoor and outdoor air over time. A closed room is therefore not a sealed or source-free room. [Peer-reviewed indoor/outdoor fiber study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27989388/) and [EPA: outdoor air enters schools](https://www.epa.gov/iaq-schools/why-indoor-air-quality-important-schools).

4. **AIR MOVEMENT AND SETTLING [SCIENCE]:** Very small or light fibers and fragments can remain suspended for a time, travel with moving air, and be lifted again when people walk, clean, or use a fan. Gravity eventually deposits many particles onto floors, desks, dust, or an open collection surface. USGS found plastic, mostly fibers, on more than 90 percent of atmospheric-deposition filters in one study, including remote sites. [USGS: It Is Raining Plastic](https://www.usgs.gov/publications/it-raining-plastic).

5. **WATER AND RETURN PATHS [SCIENCE]:** Outdoors, rain can remove particles from the air and wash pavement dust toward soil, drains, streams, and lakes. Indoors, particles may settle, be vacuumed, move through ventilation, or leave on clothing and shoes; outdoor particles can enter by those same routes. The pathway is a loop with several branches, not a one-way trip from one source to one sample. [EPA: tire-wear particles in air and stormwater](https://nepis.epa.gov/Exe/ZyPURL.cgi?Dockey=P101741T.txt).

6. **DETECTION AND UNCERTAINTY [EVIDENCE]:** Filters, clear tape, and microscopes can help campers count and describe suspected fibers or fragments by size, color, and shape. Visual evidence alone cannot prove that every particle is plastic because hair, cellulose, and other natural materials can look similar; spectroscopy is normally used to confirm polymer type. Field blanks, covered samples, clean tools, and repeated procedures help reveal contamination and uncertainty. [EPA: limits of visual microscopy](https://cfpub.epa.gov/si/si_public_record_report.cfm?Lab=CEMM&dirEntryId=361542) and [NOAA laboratory methods](https://marinedebris.noaa.gov/microplastics/laboratory-methods-analysis-microplastics-marine-environment).

7. **JUSTICE AND SYSTEM CHANGE [JUSTICE/ACTION]:** Traffic, school location, building condition, ventilation, work, housing, and access to monitoring shape who encounters airborne pollution and who has power to respond. A national study found school proximity to major roads was associated with race/ethnicity and socioeconomic indicators, although that study examined traffic pollution broadly rather than microplastics alone. Fair responses include safer product and tire design, less unnecessary plastic, better building and stormwater systems, transparent monitoring, and community authority over decisions. [Peer-reviewed U.S. school-roadway study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4179205/).

## Our Gapless Claim

> **Plastic does not have to be visible to remain present. Friction and weathering release small particles from outdoor and indoor materials. Air movement, human activity, rain, and runoff transport them; gravity and contact deposit them. Sampling can reveal suspected microplastics in both places, but careful controls and chemical confirmation are needed before we claim what each particle is.**

### What we will not claim

- **Closed is not sealed:** finding particles indoors does not prove they were created only inside the room.
- **Visible under a microscope is not chemically confirmed:** campers should report *suspected microplastics* unless polymer testing confirms them.
- **Detected is not proven harmful:** finding a particle documents possible presence or exposure, not a specific health effect. [WHO reviews the remaining health uncertainties](https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789240054608).
- **Individual choice is not system change:** families and young people cannot redesign tires, buildings, products, or public infrastructure by themselves.

## Inclusion Changes The Design

Neurodivergent, chronically ill, disabled, multilingual, culturally diverse, and multiply identified campers should not have to tolerate pain, sensory overload, chemical exposure, public speaking, or one rigid technology in order to count as scientists. [CAST UDL Guidelines 3.0](https://udlguidelines.cast.org/) emphasizes multiple and intersecting identities, multiple media, flexible communication, accessible tools, learner choice, interdependence, and challenging exclusionary practices.

## Camp Investigation Moves

1. **NOTICE [EVIDENCE]:** Compare the parking lot and classroom. Photograph or map possible sources before collecting anything.
2. **MODEL [ACCESS]:** Build a first explanation with cards, objects, drawing, speech, text, audio, or home language.
3. **INVESTIGATE [EVIDENCE]:** Collect paired indoor/outdoor deposition or surface samples plus a blank; label, cover, filter, observe, classify, and tally.
4. **REVISE [SCIENCE]:** Use source evidence to repair missing links and mark where the data support, complicate, or fail to answer the model.
5. **ADVOCATE [JUSTICE/ACTION]:** Create an image-led poster or plastic-art message for a real audience about a product, building, traffic, monitoring, or policy change.

This follows a [gapless explanation in Ambitious Science Teaching](https://ambitiousscienceteaching.org/get-started/): campers connect observable evidence to an unobservable mechanism without a "magic" jump.

## Strengthening Aastha's Activity Ideas

- **Banana and plastic comparison:** Use the jars as a multi-day observation that raises questions about biological decomposition and plastic persistence. It does not prove that plastic never changes; it shows that the two materials visibly change at different rates under the camp conditions.
- **Scale ladder:** Start at 5 millimeters, then move to micrometers and nanometers with a printed scale or zoom sequence. Keep the conversion accurate: 1 millimeter = 1,000 micrometers = 1,000,000 nanometers.
- **Tire-wear evidence:** Do not have campers sand or aerosolize an open tire sample. Use images, an instructor-prepared sealed sample, or a fully enclosed demonstration so learners with asthma, chemical sensitivity, or other health concerns are not asked to trade safety for participation.
- **Indoor/outdoor comparison:** Pair each environmental sample with a blank opened only during the collection window. Keep containers covered, record location and time, and treat microscope observations as suspected particles.
- **Process of science:** Let the poster show the initial model, evidence, contamination controls, revised model, uncertainty, and next question, not only a polished conclusion.
