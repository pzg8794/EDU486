**Current anchoring phenomenon:** **Humans aren’t trash cans, but we have plastics in our bodies.**

**Shared planning document:** [Videos/Internet Links tab](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1IFu_y_sYbNeNs1K4J_lD6KS4I30f8wxggX4SS38acRI/edit?tab=t.wwan9k5izahf)

> **Media goal:** Use one short video and a small set of trusted visual sources to help campers explain sources, movement, sampling, uncertainty, and action. A video is an entry point, not the evidence for every claim.

## Recommended Two-Minute Opener

[What Are Microplastics? Science For Kids](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fyYy58aTs7U) is a 2:06 video from the National Science Foundation. It is the strongest of Aastha's three original choices for a short opener because it is concise, youth-facing, and produced by a federal science agency.

**Before watching:** Ask campers to choose one focus: where microplastics come from, how they move, how scientists detect them, or what the video leaves unanswered.

**After watching:** Ask, "What could help explain how plastics reach human bodies, and what claim still needs evidence?"

## Other Original Video Options

- [What Are Microplastics?](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tNBFiTx6PpU) is a 2:27 video from Environment and Climate Change Canada. Use it as an alternate explanation or a source-comparison video.
- [How Long Does Plastic Take To Break Down?](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QQJbJxz5RhQ) is a 1:49 video from Porci's Ocean Patrol. Use it only to raise the smaller-versus-gone question, then check the explanation against [NOAA's fragmentation page](https://response.restoration.noaa.gov/node/1471).

Check captions, playback speed, audio level, and visual intensity before camp. Prepare a transcript, short summary, or still-image route so watching and hearing the video are never participation requirements.

## Trusted Visual And Reading Options

- [USGS microplastics slideshow](https://www.usgs.gov/media/slideshows/microplastics): public-domain images of different microplastic forms and sources.
- [NOAA: What Are Microplastics?](https://marinedebris.noaa.gov/what-are-microplastics-0): the under-5-millimeter definition and source overview.
- [Leslie et al. blood study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35367073/): chemical detection of plastic particles in a small human blood study.
- [Jenner et al. lung-tissue study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35364151/): chemical detection of microplastics in a small set of human lung-tissue samples.
- [FDA evidence boundary](https://www.fda.gov/food/environmental-contaminants-food/microplastics-and-nanoplastics-foods): detection in food and human samples does not by itself establish health risk.
- [WHO drinking-water review](https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789241516198): evidence and uncertainty for a possible water pathway.
- [EPA visual-microscopy limits](https://cfpub.epa.gov/si/si_public_record_report.cfm?Lab=CEMM&dirEntryId=361542): why a visible particle is not automatically confirmed plastic.
- [NOAA laboratory methods](https://marinedebris.noaa.gov/microplastics/laboratory-methods-analysis-microplastics-marine-environment): sampling, contamination controls, and analysis background.
- [EPA 2025 youth environmental award projects](https://www.epa.gov/education/presidents-environmental-youth-award-peya-2025-winners): youth-led examples connecting evidence, community education, and policy action.

## Accessible Media Routine

- Announce the title, source, purpose, and runtime before pressing play.
- Offer captions plus a transcript, summary, still image, or trusted reading.
- Provide headphone, lower-volume, quiet-viewing, and no-screen options.
- Pause after one idea instead of playing several videos in a row.
- Let campers respond through notes, drawing, pointing, sorting cards, speech, audio, or home language.
- Avoid surprise cold calling; provide the question before the media and processing time afterward.
- Describe important visuals aloud and never use color as the only cue.

## Media Literacy Prompts

1. Who made this, and what expertise or perspective do they bring?
2. Which claim is directly supported by evidence in the source?
3. What part of our human-body phenomenon does it help explain?
4. What uncertainty, community, identity, or system is missing?
5. What would we need to observe or measure before repeating the claim about our own samples?

## Ready-To-Use Ten-Minute Sequence

1. **LOOK, 1 minute:** Choose one image from the [USGS slideshow](https://www.usgs.gov/media/slideshows/microplastics) and record one notice and one question.
2. **WATCH OR READ, 2 minutes:** Use the [National Science Foundation opener](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fyYy58aTs7U) or its transcript/summary route.
3. **SORT, 2 minutes:** Place ideas under source, movement, settling, detection, uncertainty, or action.
4. **MODEL, 3 minutes:** Add one arrow and one evidence note to the [evidence-to-agency model](../../public-artifacts/invisible-invaders-evidence-to-agency-map.png).
5. **CHECK, 2 minutes:** Verify one claim with NOAA, FDA, WHO, or one of the peer-reviewed human-sample studies.
