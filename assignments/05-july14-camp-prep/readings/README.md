# Task 6 Reading Hub - Puzzle Plan / EQUITAS

This hub organizes the five assigned readings and the official Storylines media through Piter Garcia's Puzzle Plan goals. The Puzzle Plan connects lived experience, education, data, technology, and research so that access barriers are not misdiagnosed as limits in intelligence or scientific understanding. **EQUITAS** is the evidence-and-design lens used to ask whose knowledge counts, what context is missing, what barrier may be hiding thinking, and what needs to be redesigned.

## The Puzzle Plan Claims Driving These Notes

1. **Access barriers are not thinking barriers.** Reading speed, speech, handwriting, stamina, working memory, sensory tolerance, English dominance, or tool familiarity cannot be treated as proxies for scientific ability.
2. **Lived experience is evidence.** Youth questions, family and community knowledge, disability culture, language, and local observations belong in knowledge building.
3. **Evidence must reveal thinking.** Models, drawings, talk, text, photos, data tables, maps, audio, gesture, peer teaching, and revision can all provide rigorous evidence.
4. **Technology is judged by agency.** A tool matters when it expands participation, expression, critique, communication, or action; efficiency and novelty are not enough.
5. **Diagnosis should lead to redesign, not blame.** When participation breaks down, examine the environment, task, tool, timing, and support before locating the problem in the learner.
6. **Multiple identities shape participation.** Neurodivergence, chronic illness, disability, race, culture, language, gender, trauma, and community context interact rather than appearing one at a time.
7. **Learning should move toward repair.** Access, Identity, Healing, Agency, Criticality, Joy, and Transformation are connected outcomes, not decorative labels.

## Five Readings, Five Jobs

| Reading | Puzzle Plan theme | What it contributes | What we must press further |
|---|---|---|---|
| [Reiser et al.: Storyline Units](reiser-storyline-units.md) | **Visible coherence and epistemic agency** | Youth should know what they are figuring out, why it matters, and how each investigation advances the shared model | Avoid pseudoagency and design participation beyond fast public talk |
| [Smetana et al.: Let's Be Litter Free](smetana-lets-be-litter-free.md) | **Evidence justice from local patterns to policy** | Compare categories, datasets, claims, limits, and possible actions using community litter evidence | Move beyond equal individual responsibility toward systems, power, and unequal burdens |
| [Wisbrock et al.: Talking Trash](wisbrock-talking-trash.md) | **Multimodal literacy for public action** | Research, vocabulary, collaboration, revision, and public communication can develop together | Prevent technology expertise, reading load, or a 60-second product from hiding scientific thinking |
| [Wiggins and McTighe: Backward Design](wiggins-mctighe-backward-design.md) | **Design from understanding and valid evidence** | Name the transfer goal, decide acceptable evidence, then choose activities and tools | Make evidence multimodal and context-aware so assessment does not reproduce diagnostic injustice |
| [Ippolito et al.: Science and Literacy Together](ippolito-science-literacy-together.md) | **Expand what counts as scientific text and expertise** | Doing, talking, reading, and writing science can form one inquiry system | Do not replace a print-only hierarchy with another narrow hierarchy of acceptable communication |

## Combined Design Rule For Invisible Invaders

Start with the indoor/outdoor suspected-microplastics phenomenon and a justice-centered transfer goal. Let youth questions shape the investigation while keeping the path visible. Build equivalent ways to observe, research, model, communicate, and revise; gather evidence across those modes; then use the evidence to support public action that addresses systems and unequal burdens rather than blaming individuals.
