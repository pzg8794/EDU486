# Ippolito et al. - Learning Science and Literacy Together

**Puzzle Plan theme:** Expand what counts as scientific text and expertise
**Citation:** Ippolito, J., Condie, C., Blanchette, J., & Cervoni, C. (2018). Teaching teachers: Learning science and literacy together. *Science and Children, 56*(4), 91-95.
**Direct sources:** [NSTA full article page](https://www.nsta.org/learning-science-and-literacy-together) | [Journal issue and metadata](https://www.tandfonline.com/toc/usch20/56/4)

## Read This First

The article describes a four-week coastal summer program where elementary students learn science and literacy through authentic inquiry while teachers receive daily, practice-connected professional learning. Students do science, talk science, read science, and write science; teachers use student work and classroom dilemmas as evidence for revising instruction. The Puzzle Plan connection is direct: youth labeled as struggling readers can demonstrate growing expertise when the environment gives reading and communication a scientific purpose.

## What The Source Actually Contributes

- The program served rising grades 1-5 students who had not met reading benchmarks, predominantly English learners, through local coastal investigations and teacher learning (pp. 91-92).
- Disciplinary literacy includes doing, talking, reading, and writing science, and it expands "text" to field guides, data representations, handbooks, transcripts, observations, and other scientific forms (pp. 92-93).
- Youth collect weather and harbor data, analyze mentor weather reports, draft and revise their own reports, evaluate sources, corroborate information, and defend emerging ideas with evidence (pp. 92-94).
- Teachers use daily dilemmas, student work, and discussion as formative evidence, becoming coaches who revise instruction rather than simply delivering knowledge (pp. 93-95).
- The authors report growth in science knowledge and an average of three to four months of reading growth during four weeks, while carefully describing inquiry-based instruction as a possible contributor rather than a proven sole cause (p. 94).

> "Teachers described needing to 'get out of the way' of student learning" (p. 95).

## Puzzle Plan / EQUITAS Interpretation

**Access:** Literacy difficulty in one context does not establish weak scientific thinking. Purposeful talk, observation, images, data, collaborative research, mentor texts, and revision can reveal knowledge hidden by isolated reading tasks.

**Identity:** Local harbor knowledge, language, community work, and student wonderings become resources for disciplinary participation. The Puzzle Plan asks teachers to include disability culture, family knowledge, migration, race, health, and place without forcing any student to disclose private information.

**Healing:** Becoming an author, investigator, and source evaluator can interrupt a deficit identity such as "struggling reader." Confidence here is not empty praise; it grows from producing and defending meaningful evidence.

**Agency and joy:** Students generate questions, work in research teams, compare contradictory sources, create reports, and become more autonomous. Curiosity and authentic audience give reading and writing a reason beyond compliance.

**Diagnostic justice:** Teachers treat dilemmas and student work as data about instruction. This is the learning-scientist move in the Puzzle Plan: diagnose the relationship among learner, task, tool, language, and environment, then redesign before blaming the learner.

## Invisible Invaders Design Moves

1. Treat microscope images, sample labels, tally tables, causal models, maps, source excerpts, captions, and advocacy art as scientific texts that youth can read and author.
2. Structure each investigation so youth can **do, talk, read, and represent** science, with home-language and non-speech routes available.
3. Use a source comparison routine: What does each source claim? What evidence does it use? Where do sources agree, disagree, or leave uncertainty?
4. Gather formative evidence from questions, model changes, group talk, drawings, pointing, sorting, data records, and exit choices, not only a final paragraph.
5. Hold a brief co-leader debrief after each day: Which barrier hid thinking? Whose expertise became visible? What should we redesign tomorrow?

## Tension To Carry Into Tuesday

Expanding scientific literacy must not create a new hierarchy where youth are valued only when they approximate a narrow image of a professional scientist. The Puzzle Plan keeps the disciplinary purpose while widening legitimate language, bodymind, community knowledge, and expression. "Getting out of the way" does not mean withdrawing support; it means coaching responsively, sharing authority, and redesigning from evidence.
