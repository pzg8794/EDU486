# Reiser et al. - Storyline Units

**Puzzle Plan theme:** Visible coherence without pseudoagency
**Citation:** Reiser, B. J., Novak, M., McGill, T. A. W., & Penuel, W. R. (2021). Storyline units: An instructional model to support coherence from the students' perspective. *Journal of Science Teacher Education, 32*(7), 805-829.
**Direct sources:** [Open-access article](https://doi.org/10.1080/1046560X.2021.1884784) | [What Are Storylines?](https://www.nextgenstorylines.org/what-are-storylines) | [Official embedded overview video](https://player.vimeo.com/video/350820566)

## Read This First

Storylines are not simply lessons connected by a topic. Each step should arise from a question the classroom community recognizes as necessary for explaining a phenomenon or solving a problem. Students and teachers share responsibility for building, testing, and revising the explanation, so youth can say what they are working on, why they are doing it, and what the class needs to figure out next.

## What The Source Actually Contributes

- **Coherence from the learner's perspective:** a scientifically logical sequence is insufficient if only the teacher understands why one activity follows another (pp. 805-808).
- **Epistemic agency:** youth become collaborating authors who help identify questions, strategies, next steps, and explanations rather than only performing directions (pp. 807-810).
- **Four connected routines:** anchoring phenomenon, navigation, putting pieces together, and problematizing make the knowledge-building path visible and revisable (pp. 811-823).
- **Equity is an enactment problem:** eliciting ideas is not enough. Teachers must lower social risk, challenge whose ideas are discounted, honor different discourse styles, and create norms where the community authors the consensus (pp. 808-810, 820-824).
- **Agency has a real tension:** a planned curriculum can create pseudoagency when teachers ask students to choose a direction that has already been decided. The authors argue for honest co-construction rather than pretending no plan exists (pp. 824-825).

> "Students can be collaborators in the process of identifying where they need to go" (p. 808).

## Official Page And Video Takeaway

The official Storylines page and its embedded video, *Helping Teachers Bring NGSS to Life with Next Generation Science Storylines*, frame Storylines as a way to make three-dimensional science learning purposeful from the learner's perspective. The page offers a simple diagnostic: youth should answer "What are you working on?" and "Why are you working on this?" by naming the question or problem, not by saying the teacher assigned it. That test aligns strongly with the Puzzle Plan because a hidden instructional path adds executive-function and working-memory burden that can be mistaken for disengagement or weak understanding.

## Puzzle Plan / EQUITAS Interpretation

**Access:** Visible questions, navigation records, and model histories externalize the learning path. This reduces unnecessary memory and inference demands without reducing the scientific challenge.

**Identity:** Related phenomena from home, community, disability experience, language, and culture can change the classroom's localized version of the storyline. These contributions must affect the knowledge-building record rather than being collected and ignored.

**Healing and agency:** Lowering the social risk of being wrong can interrupt shame, masking pressure, and the expectation that scientific competence must look like immediate public speech. Youth need private think time and multiple contribution routes before consensus discussion.

**Criticality:** The paper's discussion of epistemic injustice and pseudoagency asks who is believed, whose question changes the plan, and whether "choice" is genuine. This is an EQUITAS evidence question, not merely a participation technique.

**Transformation:** The storyline ends in a model, explanation, or design that the class can use. For the Puzzle Plan, that product should also support communication, advocacy, redesign, or repair.

## Invisible Invaders Design Moves

1. Begin with the discrepancy: suspected particles may appear in both parking-lot and classroom samples. Let youth notice, model, and raise questions before presenting the full causal explanation.
2. Build a question board that accepts sticky notes, drawing, dictated questions, home language, partner contributions, and private digital entry. Do not make fast whole-group speech the gate to agenda setting.
3. End each investigation with two visible records: **what we figured out** and **what we still need to know**. Use those records to justify the next activity.
4. Preserve model versions so revision is visible. A changed idea is evidence of learning, not failure.
5. Be honest about the planned materials and time boundaries. Youth can still identify the intellectual need, choose among meaningful routes, add related phenomena, and shape the public action.

## Tension To Carry Into Tuesday

Storylines can increase agency, but a question board alone does not redistribute authority. I need to watch whose ideas are repeated, credited, placed on the board, investigated, or quietly dropped. The Puzzle Plan pushes me to gather that participation evidence and redesign the routine before interpreting silence, fatigue, delayed processing, home-language thinking, or tool friction as lack of interest.
