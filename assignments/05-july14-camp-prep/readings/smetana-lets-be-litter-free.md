# Smetana et al. - Let's Be Litter Free

**Puzzle Plan theme:** Evidence justice from local patterns to policy
**Citation:** Smetana, L., Wisbrock, L., Zaker, M., & Hoellein, T. (2019). Let's Be Litter Free: Investigating anthropogenic litter in local ecosystems. *Science Scope, 43*(3), 42-52.
**Direct source:** [NSTA article page](https://www.nsta.org/lets-be-litter-free)

## Read This First

The unit turns visible local litter into a middle-grades investigation of environmental systems, data, and action. Students move from noticing schoolyard and waterway litter to studying production trends, analyzing public cleanup data, comparing classification systems, checking whether evidence can support a claim, and creating a public message. Its strongest contribution to the Puzzle Plan is that it teaches students to ask not only what the data show, but also what the data cannot establish.

## What The Source Actually Contributes

- The authors identify enduring understandings about litter's persistence, collective responsibility, measurement, environmental effects, and the possibility of legal, cultural, and behavioral change (pp. 42-43).
- Students analyze plastic production and waste graphs, public cleanup records, and patterns across location and time rather than relying only on a one-day cleanup (pp. 44-46).
- Sorting the same evidence by **use** and by **material** demonstrates that categories shape what becomes visible. Groups can produce different graphs because they interpret categories differently (pp. 47-48).
- Hypothetical policy scenarios require students to decide whether their dataset can answer a question and to name additional evidence when it cannot (pp. 49-50).
- The article includes concrete field safety guidance and a photo-based alternative using previously collected trash, so outdoor collection is not the only route to rigorous analysis (pp. 43, 46-47).

> "Measuring litter (what, where, how much) is important because it helps determine future courses of action, policy, and management" (p. 42).

## Puzzle Plan / EQUITAS Interpretation

**Access:** The photo-data alternative is not a lesser activity. It can protect youth affected by pain, fatigue, mobility limits, allergies, sensory distress, heat, or unsafe terrain while preserving categorization, graphing, comparison, and claim evaluation.

**Identity and community evidence:** Local cleanup data and community partners position the neighborhood as a knowledge site. The Puzzle Plan extends this by asking how family practices, housing, transportation, disability access, local labor, and community histories influence what waste appears and who can participate in cleanup systems.

**Evidence justice:** Categories are not neutral. A material-based table can support one claim while a use-based table supports another, and neither proves whether a policy caused change without baseline data. This directly supports EQUITAS work on missing context, biased measurement, and overconfident diagnosis.

**Criticality:** The article says responsibility is collective, but collective does not mean equal. Producers, institutions, governments, consumers, waste workers, and communities have different power, choices, benefits, and exposure.

**Transformation:** Measurement should inform policy, management, and community action. The strongest endpoint is not simply "pick up your trash" but a defensible proposal tied to the scale and limits of the evidence.

## Invisible Invaders Design Moves

1. Compare parking-lot, classroom, and blank samples while preserving location, date, method, and possible contamination as context.
2. Let youth classify suspected particles in more than one way, such as shape, color, suspected source, or location, then compare what each scheme reveals and hides.
3. Ask a claim-limit question after every graph: **Can these data answer this question? What evidence is missing?**
4. Offer equivalent analysis routes using physical samples, covered samples, enlarged still images, printed photo cards, and shared microscope images.
5. Connect proposed action to systems such as product design, textiles, ventilation, food and water systems, cleaning, waste policy, research, monitoring, and purchasing rather than assigning equal blame to every person.

## Tension To Carry Into Tuesday

The unit is strong on data literacy and practical action, but its language about shared responsibility can slide into individual behaviorism. The Puzzle Plan requires me to preserve youth agency while also asking who designed the system, who profits, who has meaningful alternatives, whose labor manages the waste, and which communities bear exposure. A justice-centered claim needs both the environmental pattern and the power context.
