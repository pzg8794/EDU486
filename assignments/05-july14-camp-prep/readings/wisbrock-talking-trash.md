# Wisbrock et al. - Talking Trash

**Puzzle Plan theme:** Multimodal scientific literacy for public action
**Citation:** Wisbrock, L., Reynolds, E., Mertins, J., Schultz, A., Hoellein, T., & Smetana, L. (2020). Talking Trash: A human problem with human solutions. *Science and Children, 57*(8), 29-35.
**Direct source:** [NSTA article page](https://www.nsta.org/science-and-children/science-and-children-aprilmay-2020/talking-trash)

## Read This First

Talking Trash uses a concrete local phenomenon to help fourth- and fifth-grade students research pollution and communicate possible solutions. The sequence moves through a local expert, lunch-waste data, a trash-cycle model, vocabulary development, source evaluation, collaborative synthesis, script revision, and a public service announcement. The unit shows that scientific understanding and communication can develop together, but the Puzzle Plan asks us to protect access so the final media product does not become a new test of reading speed, speech, stamina, or technology familiarity.

## What The Source Actually Contributes

- A local scientist and actual river trash make the problem authentic and generate student questions; the expert later returns to respond to the finished work (pp. 29-30, 34).
- Youth tally lunch waste, scale the pattern across classes and grades, and model possible trash pathways through school, landfill, recycling, rivers, organisms, oceans, and people (pp. 30-31).
- Vocabulary posters, sentence starters, guided questions, heterogeneous groups, and teacher-supported synthesis are built into the research process, with explicit attention to English learners and students receiving special education services (pp. 31-33).
- Technology is optional rather than the learning goal. The authors state that paper articles can support the same research and live presentations can substitute for digital PSAs (pp. 32, 34).
- Students use a rubric and criteria-for-success checklist, rehearse, revise a script to sixty seconds, present to an audience, and self-assess their result (pp. 33-34).

> "Trash is a concrete example of pollution, something that students can easily see and relate to" (p. 29).

## Puzzle Plan / EQUITAS Interpretation

**Access:** The article already includes print alternatives, sentence stems, flexible grouping, and live presentation. The Puzzle Plan extends this to audio-only, captioned slides, pre-recorded narration, shared speaking, image sequence, communication board, or a partner-supported performance.

**Identity:** What families buy, reuse, discard, or can afford is shaped by culture, income, time, disability, transportation, food access, and institutional choices. Youth should not be shamed or praised as if every household has equal options.

**Healing and joy:** Humor, music, props, visual effects, and group creativity can make advocacy joyful. Participation must remain identity-safe, with no forced disclosure about family consumption, diagnosis, health, or finances.

**Agency:** Students choose a habitat, research across modes, teach peers how to use tools, decide what evidence matters, and communicate to an audience. Agency becomes stronger when youth can also influence the question, role, medium, language, and requested action.

**Criticality:** A persuasive PSA combines evidence and emotion, but a polished product can hide weak reasoning or unequal labor. Assessment should separate scientific explanation, source use, collaboration, and media production rather than treating polish as understanding.

## Invisible Invaders Design Moves

1. Use enlarged sample images and a short expert or trusted-source prompt to ground the indoor/outdoor phenomenon without asking youth to handle unsafe materials.
2. Build a visible vocabulary wall with words, images, home-language equivalents, examples, and nonexamples: particle, fiber, fragment, blank, contamination, source, evidence, and uncertainty.
3. Provide a research path in video, audio, short text, image, and teacher-read forms. Keep a paper route available when screens add sensory or working-memory load.
4. Let teams create art, audio, poster, captioned video, live explanation, or a mixed format for the showcase. Use shared scientific criteria across formats.
5. Assess the explanation separately from editing skill, speech fluency, speed, handwriting, or comfort on camera.

## Tension To Carry Into Tuesday

The article's supports are useful, but some are framed as modifications for labeled groups after the core task is designed. The Puzzle Plan treats bodymind, language, energy, and technology variability as normal Tier 1 conditions. I should build multiple rigorous routes from the start and then use participation evidence to redesign the environment rather than waiting for a learner to fail or disclose a diagnosis.
