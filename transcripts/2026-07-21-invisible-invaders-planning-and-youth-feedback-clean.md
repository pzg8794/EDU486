## What Changed

**We are keeping the Invisible Invaders anchor and storyline, but changing how campers experience them.** The July 21 feedback confirmed that long explanations, required reading aloud, and one fixed response mode would not work well. Our plan now uses short demonstrations, movement, friendly challenges, visible examples, choice, proactive check-ins, and opportunities for campers to explain or show what they learned in their own words.

**Field Friday is now a concrete before-during-after investigation rather than a general jar activity.** Campers first connect the beach to their own experiences and emotions, watch a brief facilitator demonstration, predict what they will find, inspect a prepared model sample, compare visible and magnified evidence, collect an authentic sand sample, and leave one question for the camp storyline.

## What Youth And Staff Taught Us

**Keep teacher talk short and get campers doing something quickly.** Use a demonstration, image, or video segment of five minutes or less, then move into investigation. Give one direction at a time and make the full sequence visible before starting.

**Offer real choices without lowering the science goal.** Campers may read independently, ask for help, listen, point, draw, write, dictate, photograph, work with a partner, or pass and re-enter. Reading aloud and presenting to the whole group remain optional. Facilitators circulate and offer help because some campers may not ask even when they are confused.

**Use movement, social connection, and friendly challenge as learning supports.** The challenge should reward noticing, explaining, revising, helping, and completing a scientific mission rather than speed, reading fluency, or guessing the "right" number. Every day should include something campers can show a friend and explain in their own words.

**Prepare both a starting example and room for original design.** A visible model, illustration, or partially completed example can reduce task-initiation load. Campers who want to create from scratch can do so, while campers who need a starting point can adapt the example.

**Plan for different pacing.** Every activity needs a meaningful extension for early finishers, such as photographing evidence, helping with labels, comparing a second sample, checking the model against a source, or helping a peer who wants support.

## Field Friday: Final Team Plan

### Before Campers Arrive

1. Prepare **four controlled model jars** labeled MODEL A-D. Use clean sand and pre-counted, recoverable plastic proxy pieces; do not grind plastic or release pieces at the beach.
2. Pack **three empty field-sample jars**, one per expected camper, plus **four backup jars**. The working total is **eleven jars**.
3. Pack **at least three small trays**, three clean spoons or metal scoops, three magnifiers, clipboards as tray backups, written/symbol labels, pencils, a timer, and a clean transport bin.
4. Prepare a one-page **BEFORE -> DURING -> AFTER** response card and an emotional thermometer. Every prompt must allow pointing, drawing, speaking, writing, dictation, or a facilitator scribe.
5. Test the demonstration first. Use visible pieces that are easy to count, plus smaller recoverable evidence that becomes easier to notice with magnification. Keep a known-count card for each model.
6. Choose the actual field-sampling sites **at the beach** with instructors. Do not name model jars after neighborhoods or present prepared samples as beach evidence.

### During The 40-Minute Team Session

| Time | Move | What campers do | What facilitators protect |
| ---: | --- | --- | --- |
| 0-5 min | **Beach connection and emotional check-in** | Share, point to, draw, or pass on what they enjoy at the beach and how hidden pollution makes them feel | Relationships first; no forced body or health disclosure |
| 5-10 min | **Brief facilitator demonstration** | Watch how to label, pour over a tray, look, count, magnify, and record | Show the process without giving away the explanation |
| 10-25 min | **Before-during-after model investigation** | Predict, count visible pieces, use magnification, compare with the known count, and explain what changed | Friendly mission, short directions, proactive help, no reward for accuracy alone |
| 25-35 min | **Authentic sand collection** | Choose a site with the team, collect one consistent sample, label it, and record place/time/conditions | Samples stay traceable; suspected particles are not called confirmed microplastics |
| 35-40 min | **Show and question** | Show one observation or model move and leave one question for Monday | Speaking is optional; preserve campers' exact words and chosen mode |

### The Three Investigation Prompts

1. **BEFORE:** How many pieces do you think you will notice first? What makes you think that?
2. **DURING:** What can you see now? What becomes easier to notice with the magnifier?
3. **AFTER:** What changed between your prediction and the evidence? How might plastic have reached or become hidden in a real place?

**The reveal is not a trick.** Campers are told that the models were built by the team and have known counts. The real beach sample has no known answer, which is why consistent sampling, careful labels, contamination notes, and honest uncertainty matter.

## Daily Camp Design Pattern

**Use the same predictable structure every day so campers spend their energy on science rather than guessing what comes next.**

1. **PREVIEW:** Show the visual agenda, goal, participation routes, and time.
2. **CONNECT:** Begin with a familiar place, object, feeling, question, or prior idea.
3. **DO:** Move into a hands-on investigation or design task quickly.
4. **NOTICE:** Give quiet time, then invite observations through several modes.
5. **EXPLAIN AND REVISE:** Connect evidence to one named Gotta-Have and revise the model.
6. **SHOW:** Let campers share an artifact, observation, or explanation with a friend or audience.
7. **CLOSE:** Recognize contribution, preview what comes next, and end on time.

**Each day also needs one friendly mission, one movement route, one seated or low-energy route, one example for task initiation, one meaningful extension, and one proactive facilitator check-in.** These are inclusion commitments, not optional extras.

## Safety And Evidence Corrections

**Do not crush, sand, scrape, or grind plastic for the activity.** Do not seed Charlotte Beach sand or allow model pieces to escape outdoors. Use prepared, recoverable pieces in controlled containers, and use sealed photos or known-count cards if wind makes opening a model unsafe.

**Do not rinse model plastic or suspected field particles into a drain.** Return all prepared pieces to a labeled container, keep field samples separate, and follow instructor procedures for sample storage and disposal.

**Use accurate evidence language.** Say "suspected fiber or fragment" unless an appropriate method confirms the material. Detection in an environmental, food, animal, or human sample does not by itself establish the exact route, amount of exposure, or health effect.
